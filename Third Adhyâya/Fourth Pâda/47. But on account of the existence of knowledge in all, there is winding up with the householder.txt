47. But on account of the existence (of knowledge) in all, there is winding up with the householder.
As knowledge belongs to the members of all  sramas it belongs to the householder also, and for this reason the Upanishad winds up with the latter. This winding up therefore is meant to illustrate the duties (not of the householder only, but) of the members of all  sramas. Analogously in the text under discussion (Bri. Up. III, 5) the clause 'A Br hmana having risen above the desire for sons, the desire for wealth, and the desire for worlds, wanders about as a mendicant,' intimates duties belonging exclusively to the condition of the wandering beggar, and then the subsequent clause 'therefore let a Br hmana having done with learning,' &c., enjoins p nditya, b lya, and mauna (not as incumbent on the p rivr gaka only, but) as illustrating the duties of all  sramas.--This the next S tra explicitly declares.
